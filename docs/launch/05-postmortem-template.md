# Day-27 post-mortem template

Publish on **Thursday June 5, 2026 at 09:00 ET** as the third Substack
post. Cross-post to dev.to, Indie Hackers, and a Show HN follow-up
(`Show HN: OTI's first 30 days — what worked, what didn't`).

The pattern is from Citrini's Day-30 (Apricitas + Toolstem also ran
this format). The credibility currency is **brutal honesty about real
numbers** — including the bad ones. The post is what gets the
engagement-second-spike that compounds into the second 30 days.

---

## Title

```
OTI's first 30 days — what worked, what didn't, and the corpus question
```

---

## Skeleton

(Roughly 2,500 words. Fill each section with the actual data from
PostHog / GitHub / Substack / Plausible.)

### Opening (200 words)

What you set out to do. The thesis. One paragraph on "memory not
prediction" framing. End the opener with the actual headline number
— "we wanted X, we got Y."

### What happened, by the numbers (300 words + table)

```
Day 0: shipped v0.5 to oti.dev
Day 4: Show HN at 08:30 ET

30-day numbers (final):
  GitHub stars:                XXX (target: 150)
  Substack subscribers:        XXX (target: 100)
  Bluesky followers:           XXX (target: 50)
  Vercel uniques (rolling 7d): XXX (target: 1k)
  MCP installs detected:       XXX (target: 20)
  Substack Recommendations:    XXX (target: 3)
  Tier-1 citations:            XXX (target: 1)
  Podcast pitches in flight:   XXX (target: 1)
```

Be specific. Don't round generously. If the number is zero, the number
is zero.

### What worked (500 words)

3-4 specific things that drove disproportionate signups / stars /
attention. **Be concrete about WHY each worked.** Vague claims ("X
generated buzz") are dead air.

Likely candidates (fill in the actual top 3):
- Show HN front-page hour and the [specific argument] in the body
- The negative-analogue framing — readers self-identified as "yes that's the question I was asking"
- The Treasury-curve essay's specific Citrini-pattern thesis + neologism
- The MCP server in Claude Desktop being the unexpected click-through
- Substack Recommendations from [specific publication] driving the largest subscriber bolus

### What didn't work (500 words)

3-4 things you tried that flopped. **Same brutal honesty** — flopping
is data. The post is more credible when you list the failures than
when you spin them as "learning experiences."

Likely candidates (fill in the actual top 3):
- The X cross-post strategy if X traffic was thin (post-Feb 2026 URL
  pricing means you can't share links cheaply on X)
- The MCP directory submissions if they drove zero installs that
  weren't health-check bots
- The pitch to [specific big-fish Substack] that didn't reciprocate
- The friend-upvotes ask if HN caught it as a vote ring
- The Reddit cross-posts if they got auto-removed for self-promotion

### The corpus question (400 words)

The most important section. The corpus is the moat. Address head-on:

- How many community PRs did you get? (Probably 0-2.)
- Was the 20-event bounty enough incentive? Probably not — most
  open-source contributors don't want lifetime API access; they want
  something more concrete (cite, paid retainer, swag).
- Has the corpus changed materially in 30 days? Or is it still 39?
- What's the path to 75 events by v0.7?

Be honest about whether the corpus moat is REAL or just a story you
tell yourself. If you've done all the corpus expansion yourself, that's
not a moat — that's a single point of failure.

### What I'd do differently (400 words)

If you ran the 30 days again. Specific moves you'd swap, days you'd
re-order, friends you'd ask to upvote, essays you'd write differently.

Reader-empathy: the people reading this are themselves indie founders
trying to launch. The "here's what I'd do differently" is the highest-
leverage paragraph in the whole post.

### What's next (200 words)

The 60-day plan. Three concrete moves. Don't pre-promise; commit to
the ones you'll actually do.

The natural next moves after a 30-day launch:
- Custom-corpus support (defensible workflow for paying users)
- A specific buyside channel ("partner with one fund to publish their analogue brief weekly")
- Vision input (paste-a-chart-extract-regime; v0.7's marquee feature)
- Multiagent + Outcomes migration (cuts feature velocity once running)

### Closing (100 words)

Thank the contributors / commenters / Substack-recommenders by handle.
This is your social-graph receipt. People who got named publicly will
be more likely to show up for your next launch.

End with the URL: oti.dev / github.com/Al033/OTI.

## What this template buys you

Three things:

1. **Compounded second-spike traffic.** People who read the original
   essay #2 + #3 will check back for the post-mortem. The 30-day
   timestamp is their "is this still active" signal.

2. **Earned credibility for v0.7.** When you ship the next big thing,
   you're not pitching from zero — you're pitching from "here's the
   indie-finance-tool founder who does honest post-mortems."

3. **Latent virality.** Indie Hackers and dev.to specifically reward
   post-mortem format. A 30-day-retrospective post on Indie Hackers
   has a ~3× upvote rate vs an equivalent launch announcement.

---

## What to NOT do in the post-mortem

- Don't make up numbers. PostHog and GitHub are auditable; if you fudge,
  someone will catch it.
- Don't blame the audience. "Macro Twitter just doesn't get it" is a
  tell that you're losing the framing battle.
- Don't pivot mid-post-mortem. Closing with "actually OTI is now a
  different product" undoes 30 days of credibility.
- Don't apologise. The post is honest, not contrite.
